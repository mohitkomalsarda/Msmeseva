Overdraft (OD) Facility

Working Capital | Revolving Credit | Secured & Unsecured

What is Overdraft?

An Overdraft is a revolving credit facility where your bank account is linked to a sanctioned credit limit. You can draw funds up to this limit multiple times and interest is charged only on the daily closing outstanding balance — not on the full sanctioned limit. This makes it very cost-efficient for businesses with fluctuating working capital needs.

How It Works

  • Bank sanctions a limit based on your financials, collateral, and credit history
  • You can draw and repay any number of times within the limit
  • Interest charged daily on closing balance at annual rate ÷ 365
  • Interest debited to account monthly
  • Limit reviewed and renewed annually upon submission of financials

Secured vs Unsecured OD

  • Secured OD: Against FD, property, stocks, debtors — interest 8–13% p.a. Lower risk for bank → lower rate
  • Unsecured OD (Clean OD): Based on business vintage, turnover, and CIBIL score — interest 14–22% p.a.

Types of OD Facilities

  • FD OD: Against fixed deposit — cheapest, FD rate + 1–2%
  • Cash Credit (CC): Against stocks and debtors — most common for traders/manufacturers
  • Book Debt OD: Against outstanding receivables/invoices
  • Swap OD: Short-term, rolled over against expected inflows — used by exporters
  • Property OD: Against residential or commercial property — higher limits available
💡 When to use OD? Ideal for seasonal businesses, invoice-heavy industries, and businesses needing quick short-term credit. Avoid using OD for long-term capital expenditure — use Term Loans instead.

Term Loan

CAPEX | MTL | Property Purchase | Fixed Tenure

What is a Term Loan?

A Term Loan is a lump-sum disbursement for a specific purpose, repaid over a fixed period through regular instalments (monthly, quarterly, or half-yearly). The repayment schedule — either EMI or EPI — is defined at the start of the loan.

EMI vs EPI

FeatureEMI (Equated Monthly Instalment)EPI (Equated Principal Instalment)
Monthly PaymentFixed throughout tenureDecreasing each month
Interest ComponentHigher initially, decreasesDecreases each month
Principal ComponentLower initially, increasesFixed every month
Total Interest OutgoHigherLower
Cash Flow PlanningEasier (fixed amount)Variable (lower over time)

Types of Term Loans for MSMEs

  • CAPEX Loan: Capital expenditure — machinery, equipment, factory building. Tenure 3–10 years. Primary security: asset financed.
  • Medium-Term Loan (MTL): Expansion, renovation, working capital gap. Tenure 1–5 years.
  • Property Purchase Loan: For buying commercial/industrial property. Up to 15–20 years. 75–80% LTV. Property mortgaged.
  • Project Loan: For greenfield / large expansion projects. Long tenure. Multiple disbursements during construction.

Secured vs Unsecured

  • Secured: Asset financed serves as primary security; collateral property for additional cover
  • Unsecured: Based on CIBIL, turnover, vintage — available from NBFCs and fintechs at 15–24% p.a.
  • CGTMSE can be used to make secured loans collateral-free for MSEs

Working Capital Term Loan (WCTL)

Fixed Working Capital | 1–5 Years | EMI / EPI

What is WCTL?

A Working Capital Term Loan (WCTL) is sanctioned when a business has a permanent working capital deficit that cannot be managed through revolving CC/OD facilities. Unlike CC/OD which are revolving, WCTL is a one-time disbursement repaid over a fixed schedule of 12–60 months.

When is WCTL Needed?

  • When OD/CC limit is insufficient to cover actual working capital needs
  • During rapid business expansion with growing receivables and inventory
  • To fund long-credit-cycle industries (construction, project-based businesses)
  • Post-COVID re-stocking or restart situations (also eligible under ECLGS)

Key Terms

  • Tenure: 12–60 months typically (varies by bank)
  • Security: Hypothecation of current assets (stocks, debtors) as primary; collateral may be needed
  • Interest: Floating, linked to bank's MCLR or Repo Rate
  • Repayment: EMI or EPI — decided at time of sanction
  • Often granted alongside existing CC/OD as supplementary facility

Lease Rental Discounting (LRD)

Commercial Property | Rental-Backed Loan | Long Tenure

What is LRD?

LRD is a unique loan product where a property owner pledges future lease rental receivables from their commercial tenants as security. The bank discounts these future cash flows at present value and provides a lump-sum loan. The EMIs are directly repaid from the tenant's rental payments through NACH/ECS — making it essentially self-servicing.

How LRD Works

  • You own a commercial property (office/shop/warehouse) leased to a tenant
  • Bank takes assignment of lease rentals — tenant pays directly to bank's NACH
  • Loan = 70–80% of Net Present Value of remaining lease rentals
  • EMI automatically deducted from rental inflows each month
  • Tenure tied to lease agreement (typically 5–15 years)

Secured vs Unsecured

  • Fully Secured — both the property and lease rental assignment as collateral
  • Interest rates typically lower than normal LAP because cash flows are predictable
  • Tenant creditworthiness is a major factor in bank's appraisal
  • Strong tenants (MNCs, PSUs, large corps) → better rate and higher LTV

Bank Guarantee (BG)

Non-Fund Based | Contingent Liability | Tender / Performance

What is a Bank Guarantee?

A Bank Guarantee (BG) is a bank's irrevocable commitment to pay a specified amount to a beneficiary (third party) if the applicant (your business) fails to fulfil its contractual obligations. Unlike a loan, BG is a contingent liability — the bank pays only if you default, not otherwise.

Common BG Uses for MSMEs

  • Tender / Bid BG (EMD): Required to submit tenders; assures seriousness of bid. Typically 2–5% of tender value
  • Performance BG: Assures satisfactory completion of a contract/order. Typically 5–10% of contract value
  • Advance Payment BG: Issued when buyer gives advance; BG assures refund if seller fails to deliver
  • Financial / Payment BG: Guarantees payment of dues to a supplier or government body
  • Deferred Payment BG: For payment of deferred consideration (machinery purchase in instalments)

How to Avail a BG

  • Apply to your bank with application, draft BG wording, and project/contract details
  • Bank will secure with 100% cash margin OR allow issuance against your existing credit limit (fund-based)
  • BG charges: 0.5–1% p.a. on BG amount + stamp duty charges
  • BG has a defined expiry date — must be renewed if contract extends
💡 BG vs LC BG is a contingent guarantee (secondary obligation — paid only on default). LC is a primary payment mechanism (bank pays on presentation of documents). Both are non-fund based but serve different purposes.

Letter of Credit (LC)

Trade Finance | Bank-Backed Payment | Domestic & Export

What is an LC?

A Letter of Credit (LC) is a bank instrument where the buyer's bank (Issuing Bank) guarantees payment to the seller (Beneficiary) upon presentation of specified documents (invoice, bill of lading, packing list, etc.) that prove shipment or delivery has occurred. It completely eliminates credit risk between trading parties who don't know each other well.

LC Process Flow

  • Step 1: Buyer applies to their bank (Issuing Bank) for LC issuance
  • Step 2: Issuing Bank opens LC and sends it to seller's bank (Advising/Confirming Bank)
  • Step 3: Seller ships goods and prepares specified documents
  • Step 4: Seller presents documents to their bank (Negotiating Bank)
  • Step 5: Negotiating Bank pays seller after verifying documents
  • Step 6: Issuing Bank reimburses Negotiating Bank; Buyer's account debited

Types of LC

  • Sight LC: Payment on demand immediately upon document presentation
  • Usance/DA LC: Deferred payment — 30/60/90/180 days credit to buyer
  • Red Clause LC: Advance payment allowed to seller before shipment (rare)
  • Revolving LC: Automatically reinstates for repeat transactions within a period
  • Back-to-Back LC: LC opened on the strength of another (master) LC — used by trading intermediaries

LC Discounting

Trade Finance | Immediate Liquidity | Off-Balance Sheet

What is LC Discounting?

LC Discounting allows a seller/beneficiary of a Usance (deferred payment) LC to get immediate funds instead of waiting for the LC payment date. The seller "discounts" (sells) the LC receivable to a bank at a small discount (interest charge), receiving funds immediately while the bank collects payment from the issuing bank at maturity.

Benefits of LC Discounting

  • Immediate liquidity — don't wait 30/60/90 days for LC payment
  • Discounting rates typically lower than CC/OD rates (since LC-backed = bank risk)
  • For buyers: effectively extends credit period while seller gets paid immediately
  • For banks: low-risk facility since backed by another bank's guarantee

Discounting Rates

  • Domestic LC Discounting: typically 8–11% p.a.
  • Import LC Discounting: LIBOR/SOFR + spread (for foreign currency)
  • Cost depends on: creditworthiness of issuing bank, LC tenor, buyer profile

Cash Credit (CC)

Working Capital | Stock & Book Debt | Drawing Power

What is Cash Credit?

Cash Credit (CC) is India's most prevalent short-term revolving working capital facility. It is backed by the primary security of hypothecation of stocks (raw material, WIP, finished goods) and book debts (outstanding receivables/debtors). The maximum amount you can draw at any time is called the Drawing Power (DP), computed every month on submitted stock statements.

Drawing Power Calculation

DP = (Eligible Stocks × Advance Rate) + (Eligible Debtors × Advance Rate)
  • Eligible Stocks: Stocks owned by you, paid for (not consignment/hypothecated elsewhere). Aged stock (>180 days) deducted.
  • Advance Rate on Stocks: Typically 60–75% (i.e., bank applies 25–40% margin)
  • Eligible Debtors: Debtors below 90 days. Old/doubtful debtors excluded.
  • Advance Rate on Debtors: Typically 60–75%
  • DP cannot exceed the overall CC sanctioned limit

Monthly Obligations

  • Submit Monthly Stock Statement (MSS) by 7th of every month
  • Monthly Book Debt Statement with ageing
  • Annual renewal with audited financials
  • Quarterly progress reports for larger limits

Standby Letter of Credit (SBLC)

International Trade | Contingent Guarantee | Last Resort Payment

What is SBLC?

An SBLC (Standby Letter of Credit) is a payment guarantee used primarily in international trade. Unlike a regular LC which is a primary payment mechanism, SBLC is invoked only when the buyer/applicant FAILS to fulfil contractual obligations — making it a "standby" or "last resort" payment guarantee. It is governed by ISP98 (International Standby Practices) or UCP600.

SBLC vs Regular LC

FeatureRegular LCSBLC
Primary usePayment mechanism for tradeGuarantee / credit enhancement
When paidOn document presentation (normal)Only on buyer's default

MSME Use Cases

  • International contracts where foreign party wants payment security
  • Cross-border borrowing where Indian bank issues SBLC for overseas lender
  • Oil, gas, commodity trading contracts
  • Long-term supply agreements with foreign buyers

LER — Loan Equivalent Risk

Banking Risk Metric | Off-Balance Sheet Exposure

What is LER?

LER (Loan Equivalent Risk) is a regulatory metric used by banks to quantify and manage off-balance sheet credit exposures. Off-balance sheet items like Bank Guarantees, Letters of Credit, derivatives, and undrawn credit limits create contingent credit risk. LER converts these into equivalent loan amounts for risk-weighting and large exposure calculations under RBI's Large Exposure Framework (LEF).

Why it Matters for MSMEs

  • BGs and LCs issued to you count towards the bank's exposure limit on your firm
  • Under RBI's LEF, a single borrower exposure cannot exceed 15% of bank's capital
  • High off-balance sheet exposure = reduced room for fresh fund-based limits
  • Understanding LER helps MSMEs plan their limit utilisation across banks
  • Banks may ask you to reduce contingent limits before enhancing fund-based limits
💡 Practical Tip: If you're reaching your bank's exposure limits, consider distributing facilities across 2–3 banks to create headroom for future credit growth.

Commercial LAP — Loan Against Property

Commercial / Industrial Property | 55–65% LTV | Up to 15 Years

What is Commercial LAP?

Commercial LAP is a secured loan against commercial or industrial property — shops, offices, warehouses, factory buildings, or industrial plots. It offers significantly higher loan amounts and longer repayment tenures compared to unsecured loans, making it ideal for MSME business expansion, debt consolidation, or large working capital infusion.

Key Parameters

ParameterCommercial LAPResidential LAP
LTV (Loan-to-Value)55–65% of market value70–75% of market value
Typical TenureUp to 15 yearsUp to 20 years
Interest Rate9.5–13% p.a.8.5–12% p.a.
Processing Fee0.5–1.5%0.25–1%
Income ProofBusiness financials + ITRSalary slips / ITR

Secured or Unsecured?

  • Fully Secured — commercial property mortgaged (registered/equitable)
  • Risk: Property can be auctioned (SARFAESI) if loan defaults continuously
  • Much lower rate vs unsecured business loans (often 5–10% lower)

TReDS — Trade Receivables Discounting System

Fintech Platform | Invoice Discounting | RBI Regulated

What is TReDS?

TReDS is an RBI-regulated electronic platform that enables MSMEs to discount their receivables (invoices) raised on corporate buyers and government entities at competitive, transparent market rates. Multiple financiers bid on each invoice, ensuring best pricing.

How TReDS Works

  • MSME seller uploads invoice on TReDS platform
  • Buyer (corporate) accepts the invoice on the platform
  • Multiple banks/financiers bid — lowest rate wins
  • MSME gets funded within 1–2 days at the best bid rate
  • Buyer pays the financer on due date

TReDS Platforms in India

  • RXIL: NSE + SIDBI joint venture
  • M1xchange: Mynd Solutions (Axis Bank backed)
  • Invoicemart: A.TReDS (Axis Bank + mjunction)
📌 Note: Companies with turnover above ₹500 crore are now mandatorily required to be onboarded on TReDS platforms to ensure timely payment to MSME vendors.
